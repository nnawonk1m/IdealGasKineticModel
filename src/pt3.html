Newton's third law of motion states that for every action there exists an equal and opposite direction. In context, if there exists a force exerted by the wall on the molecule, there exists a force of equal magnitude exerted by the molecule on the wall. In Part 2: Force, we had calculated the force that the wall exerts on the gas molecule through looking at the change in momentum of the molecule before and after collision with the wall. Since we know the force the wall exerted on the molecule, using Newton's third law of motion we can determine the force the molecule exerted on the wall - the same magnitude and opposite the direction.

A: wall

B: molecule

FAB = -mcx2 / L = force exerted by A(wall) on B(molecule)

FBA = mcx2 / L = force exerted by B(molecule) on A(wall)

Until now we have considered the motion of a single molecule. However, we are able to use statistical reasoning expand our conclusion to not only one molecule but to the N gas molecules present in the container. Since there are N molecules in the container, we can express the total force exerted on the wall as:

Fx = m / L * (cx12 + cx22 + cx32 + ... + cxN2)

With so many molecules present, the velocity of the molecules can be averaged out.

cx2 = (cx12 + cx22 + cx32 + ... + cxN2) / N

(cx12 + cx22 + cx32 + ... + cxN2) = cx2 * N

Fx = m / L * cx2 * N = Nm / L * cx2

The figure shows how we can resolve the vector c(pink arrow) into its three constituents: cx(red arrow), cy(green arrow), and cz(blue arrow). Imagine a rectangular prism with edges with lengths cx, cy, and cx. Using the Pythagorean Theorem, we are able to induce the equation:

c2 = cx2 + cy2 + cz2

Going back to Part 1: Assumptions, remember the fact that there is an "equal likelikhood of a molecule moving in any direction as in any other direction." Therefore, with a statistical model, the following generalization can be made:

cx2 = cy2 = cz2

cx2 = 1/3 * c2

Fx = 1/3 * Nm / L * c2
